Economic Resilience

Economic resilience refers to an economy's ability to swiftly resume its core functions when impacted by a crisis, thereby minimising the duration during which it is unable to fulfil its core functions. This can include adapting its structures to become more resilient to future challenges.

Resilience, derived from the Latin verb "resiliere", was first introduced by Holling (1973) in ecology, who defined it as the general stability of a system and distinguished between engineering resilience, the ability of a system with one equilibrium to restore its pre-shock state quickly, and ecological resilience, the ability of a system with multiple equilibria to absorb a shock and persist in a different state. However, both definitions are static and inadequate for dynamic socio-economic systems. Consequently, the term adaptive resilience emerged to describe the positive adaptability of a system in maintaining its core functions while changing its overall structure to learn from shocks and “bounce-forward”.

Measuring what matters

Amidst mounting crises, our concept of economic resilience assesses how well economic structures can react to shocks and consistently deliver necessary goods and services at appropriate quantity and quality for society, all while upholding the broader objective of promoting intergenerational wellbeing within the limits of our planet. This involves minimising the time that the economy is away from fulfilling its purpose, as well as continuously adapting and learning from shocks to become more resilient and better prepared for future challenges.

The history of economic resilience

For a long time, economic growth has been synonymous with strength, preparedness, and recovery. However, recent discussions about resilience and recovery have shown that relying on GDP growth alone is not enough to face current and future shocks. Economic resilience has emerged as a better organising principle for policy thinking about stability and wellbeing. Unfortunately, the current definition of economic resilience is underdeveloped, and GDP growth is often used as the only benchmark. To be more comprehensive, we need to move beyond GDP and consider indicators that incorporate social and environmental aspects, as advocated by the European Commission (SWD/2013/303 final). In this context, we at ZOE Institute developed a theoretical framework to assess the resilience of economies, ensuring the goal of the economy is in line with socio-ecological boundaries.

To apply this framework to EU member states and assess their level of economic resilience, we at ZOE Institute developed the Economic Resilience Index (ERI). The ERI measures and compares the economic resilience of 25 EU member states with respect to six resilience dimensions and three resilience capacities.

The concept of economic resilience has gained popularity over the recent years. It has been used by a variety of stakeholders including the OECD, IMF, World Bank, WTO, and European Commission.

What is missing?

Against the backdrop of numerous recent crises, building economic resilience is an essential concept for economies and societies to pursue. However, as economic resilience is often equated with GDP resilience, many crucial dimensions such as environmental and social aspects are neglected. Moreover, resilience is often understood as the ability to bounce back to a pre-shock status quo. However, to be truly crisis resilient, economies must also be able to bounce forward and adapt when needed. In summary, a future-fit understanding of economic resilience that includes all relevant dimensions is needed to enable economies and societies to cope with current crises and prepare for future ones.

Developing a theoretical framework to assess the resilience of economies in the face of crises, going beyond the traditional use of GDP as a reference measure, Hafele et al., 2023a.

To address the above-mentioned blind spots of existing concepts of economic resilience, Hafele et al. develop an economic resilience framework based on a social-ecological approach to defining the economic system.

The Economic Resilience Index (ERI): assessing EU economies’ future-preparedness to thrive when faced with continuous crises, Hafele et al., 2023b.

Building on the concept of economic resilience developed in Hafele et al. (2023a), Hafele et al. construct the Economic Resilience Index (ERI) and compare the economic resilience of EU Member States with regard to six resilience dimensions.

Read here

Understanding the economy as a provisioning system to analyse the differences between levels of resource use and social outcomes, Fanning et al., 2020.

Fanning et al. explore the concept of “provisioning systems” as a set of elements working together to satisfy a human need. They go on to identify six important elements of provisioning systems (households, markets, the commons, the state, techniques, and material stock) that can be further analysed and compared.

Download here

Developing a policy framework for societal resilience in the EU, Manca et al., 2017.

Manca et al. build a conceptual framework for economic resilience. They distinguish between different resilience capacities, that are a system’s capacity to respond to shocks either by absorption, adaption, or transformation.
